<h1>Bumble bees and the physiology of heat stress</h1>

<p>
Bumble bees are essential pollinators that face increasing thermal stress as
climates warm. We study how physiology and behavior interact to determine
whether bees can tolerate rising temperatures. Our work integrates
<b>metabolism</b>, <b>water balance</b>, and <b>collective behavior</b> to
understand how both individual bees and entire colonies respond to thermal
environments.
</p>

<p>
By linking physiological mechanisms to colony function, we aim to understand
how pollinators will respond to climate change and whether collective
organization within nests can buffer bees from environmental stress.
</p>

  <div class="panel-text">
    <h2>Physiology of queens under thermal stress</h2>
    <p>
      Bumble bee queens initiate colonies alone and must survive large
      fluctuations in temperature during the earliest stages of colony
      formation. We test whether queens can acclimate to heat stress by
      adjusting core physiological traits.
    </p>

    <p>
      Our work shows that queens exposed to elevated temperatures do not
      substantially shift <b>metabolic rate</b>, <b>water loss rate</b>, or
      <b>thermal tolerance</b>. Instead, species differ in their underlying
      respiratory physiology, and some species exhibit stronger thermal
      sensitivity of metabolic rate than others.
    </p>

    <ul class="key-points">
      <li><b>Metabolic rate:</b> species differ in thermal sensitivity</li>
      <li><b>Water loss:</b> limited evidence for individual acclimation</li>
      <li><b>Respiration:</b> interspecific variation in how bees exchange gases</li>
    </ul>

    <div class="callout">
      <div class="callout-title">Key insight</div>
      <p>
        Queens show limited physiological acclimation to heat, suggesting that
        thermal tolerance may depend more on species-level traits than on
        plastic responses within individuals.
      </p>
    </div>
  </div>
</article>

  <div class="panel-text">
    <h2>Collective acclimation at the colony level</h2>
    <p>
      Because individual queens show limited physiological acclimation,
      we are investigating whether entire colonies can acclimate collectively.
      Bumble bee nests function as integrated systems in which the behavior
      of many workers shapes the internal thermal environment of the colony.
    </p>

    <p>
      We study how nests exposed to warm and cool conditions change through
      coordinated behavior, including worker movement, nest ventilation,
      and activity patterns. These experiments allow us to test whether
      colonies behave as <b>superorganisms</b> capable of adjusting their
      collective physiology to changing temperatures.
    </p>

    <ul class="key-points">
      <li><b>Nest acclimation:</b> colonies exposed to warm and cool environments</li>
      <li><b>Collective behavior:</b> coordinated activity among workers</li>
      <li><b>Colony physiology:</b> emergent responses that arise from many individuals</li>
    </ul>

    <div class="callout">
      <div class="callout-title">Big question</div>
      <p>
        Can colonies buffer pollinators from climate warming through
        collective behavior, even when individual bees show limited
        physiological flexibility?
      </p>
    </div>
