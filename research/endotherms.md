<h1>Endotherm performance and heat balance</h1>

<p>
Endotherms regulate body temperature through metabolic heat production and
heat exchange with the environment. Yet despite decades of research on
thermoregulation, there are surprisingly few widely used frameworks for
describing <b>thermal performance curves</b> in endotherms.
</p>

<p>
Our work develops a mechanistic approach that links organismal performance
to <b>heat flux between animals and their environments</b>. By focusing on
net sensible heat exchange, we can identify the environmental conditions
where endotherms operate most efficiently and predict how those conditions
shift across climates and landscapes.
</p>

</div>

  <div class="panel-text">
    <h2>Thermal performance and heat exchange</h2>
    <p>
      Thermal performance curves are widely used to describe how temperature
      shapes organismal function. However, most existing frameworks were
      developed for ectotherms and do not translate easily to endothermic
      animals that actively regulate body temperature.
    </p>

    <p>
      We propose that performance in endotherms can be understood in terms of
      <b>net sensible heat flux</b> between animals and their environments.
      When heat gain and heat loss are balanced, metabolic demands for
      thermoregulation are minimized. This point of thermal balance defines
      the conditions where performance is expected to be highest.
    </p>

    <ul class="key-points">
      <li><b>Heat exchange:</b> net sensible heat flux between organism and environment</li>
      <li><b>Thermal balance:</b> minimal thermoregulatory costs</li>
      <li><b>Optimal performance:</b> predicted to occur near heat balance</li>
    </ul>

    <div class="callout">
      <div class="callout-title">Concept</div>
      <p>
        Optimal performance in endotherms may occur where environmental heat
        exchange minimizes the energetic costs of thermoregulation.
      </p>
    </div>
  </div>

    <h2>Predicting distributions and climate responses</h2>
    <p>
      Because heat balance depends on both organismal traits and environmental
      conditions, this framework can be used to predict how endotherms perform
      across landscapes and seasons. By combining physiology with climate data,
      we can estimate where animals experience favorable thermal conditions
      and where thermoregulatory costs become limiting.
    </p>

    <p>
      This approach allows us to generate predictions about how species
      distributions shift across geographic gradients and how performance may
      change under future climate scenarios.
    </p>

    <ul class="key-points">
      <li><b>Spatial predictions:</b> mapping thermal performance across landscapes</li>
      <li><b>Temporal dynamics:</b> seasonal and daily variation in heat balance</li>
      <li><b>Climate change:</b> forecasting shifts in suitable environments</li>
    </ul>

    <div class="callout">
      <div class="callout-title">Big question</div>
      <p>
        Can heat balance provide a mechanistic link between physiology,
        climate, and the geographic distributions of endothermic animals?
      </p>
